30 Second Film about ROMANTIC LOVE

This project explores the paradox of human affection through the lens of color and time. It captures the irony of a thousand days of romantic bond collapsing in just three minutes, shifting the world from a saturated, vibrant landscape into a colorless world. By utilizing a seamless loop, the project illustrates our inevitable return to love, how we knowingly step back into the "coincidence" of a new encounter despite the certain grief that follows. It is a visual testament to the resilient human will to find color again, even after the world turns gray.

Phase Transition of Snow

This project explores the concept of Phase Transition by sonically mapping the physical transformation of snow from a solid to a liquid state through a series of textured stepping sounds. Drawing on Trevor Pinch's "Sculpting Sound" framework, the narrative begins with the high-frequency, crisp "crunch" of dry snow, which gradually dissolves into the dense, low-frequency "slush" of melting ice. To provide a cohesive narrative arc, a recording of a bus stop at 10 PM serves as the "Sonic Glue," grounding the abstract transitions in a familiar urban environment. The piece culminates in a surreal departure, where the everyday sound of a bus arrival is transformed using Space Designer and volume envelopes into a spaceship-like sonic event, shifting the listener from a mundane wintry walk into an evocative, otherworldly space.

Computing Art

The inspiration for this project began with the observation of a discarded children's slide, an object that represents a profound social and emotional contradiction. While a slide is a universal symbol of childhood joy, play, and physical memory, its state as "trash" reveals a moment of social abandonment and urban neglect. I wanted to explore the tension between this "authentically human" relic—carrying the physical traces of wear, dirt, and history—and the cold, mathematical nature of computation. By digitizing this abandoned object, the project enacts a conversion of lived material reality into a computable form, questioning whether digital preservation can ever truly recover the "aura" of the original experience.

Hack

This project hacks the most fundamental concept of our environment: silence. In most interactive art, you make noise to get a response. This project, however, hacks that expectation by making silence the active trigger for an alarm. It is an "Inversion Hack"—flipping the rules of cause and effect.
